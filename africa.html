Africa

The history of Africa is very rich, and it is scarred by foreign influence, however Africans have survived very well, and that is why they have prospered. In this continent, the many empires that have occupied it have not known about the African tribes' chivalrous spirit, and enslaved the many with their superior technology and weapons.
Our story begins in the Golden Age of Africa, where trade was starting to make merchants richer. Suddenly, the Europeans cane, trying to find a route to India (now called Bharat). This was a journey that changed the African continent forever. That is how the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade, or the Triangular Trade, began.

The Europeans became interested in making the Africans slaves and forcing them to work on plantations in the Americas after their earlier source of labor, the Native Americans, died because of disease. The Africans getting captured, put into ships, and sent to America to work on plantations. During this time, the Europeans stayed on the coast of Africa, but over time, they slowly "invaded" the rest of the continent. The technologically advanced Europeans easily took over the vast majority of Africa.
A few hundred years later, in the 1800s, the Europeans saw their numerous colonies as an opportunity for power. Those numerous colonies provided raw materials like meat, and medicinal plants, and a more economic market for the ruling country of that colony. This is called imperialism.
Fast forward to 160 years later, and the African countries were finally getting their independence! One by one, the European countries were slowly giving the Africans their independence. But the same colonial boundaries continued, tearing the countries into civil wars from the multitude of tribes in one country, and also dictatorships, when a single leader has complete power and is backed by the military.
Then, the Pan-African Movement was started, and it called for unity amongst the Africans. Their goal? They wanted people with African descent around the world to think of Africa as their homelans. The peaceful unification of Africa has not happened yet, but the Pan-African Movement was the spark of many independence movements all around Africa, and was also responsible for the creation of the African Union in 2002.
But where there seems to be peace, there is also war. This place is called Sudan. Sudan was a British colony in Africa until 1956, when it gained independence. Its SECOND civil war broke in 1983 and the country suffered nearly two million deaths. In 2011, South Sudan got independence, which perished the civil war. The region, however, still has large conflicts, and a third civil war is likely to happen in the future.




The Republic of South Africa

South Africa's history has its ups and downs but it is still rich and we can learn a lot from it. Our story starts thousands of years ago, when people living in that area were hunters, gatherers, and farmers. 2,000 years ago, those hunters, gatherers, and farmers began herding wild animals and planting various crops, which allowed development of their larger settlements.
200 years later, one of the northern tribes, the Bantu, settled into the area, and believe it or not, most South African citizens actually trace their roots back to those times!
Fast forward 1,200 years later, and look who's back: the Europeans! Portuguese explorers were the very first to colonize Africa, but all the other continents knew it (Africa) was there. They came in the 15th century (or the 1400s) but the Portuguese were trying to find a sea route to India (that was eventually found in 1497 by Vasco da Gama).
Eventually, the other empires caught in the news, and in the late 16th century (the 1500s), who came? The people who came were the British and the Dutch.
In 1652, the Dutch East India Company (a trading company who protected the Dutch government's interests in the Indian Ocean region) set up a trading post in present-day Cape Town. Five years later, those same European settlers took off trying to make permanent farms, and a century later, those European settlements began moving inland, and not just inland. FURTHER inland.
That forced the natives off their lands and into servitude (which means slavery of any kind). Even though slavery was abolished in 1834, the native Africans still were discriminated against.
After the Portuguese left South Africa, it was taken over by the British in the early 1800s, and the descendants of Dutch settlers moved north, which was Zulu territory (Zulu is a South African native tribe). This caused conflict between the Zulu and the Dutch.
In South Africa, the Dutch created two states: the first was the Transvaal, and the second was the Orange Free State. In the early 1900s, those two states merged with British South Africa to form the Union of South Africa.
The Union of South Africa, though, was a constitutional monarchy, and the South African natives were considered (by the government) to be second-class citizens.
Meanwhile, the Africans created the African National Congress (or the ANC) to work towards equality. In 1961, when South Africa changed from a monarchy to a republic, racist policies continued to be enforced, even with the government change.
There was a prison called Robben Island, and it was basically South Africa's Alcatraz. It was "the most notorious prison for anti-apartheid (the former policy of segregation in South Africa) protesters." Famously, Nelson Mandela spent 18 of his 27 years of prison at Robben Island. The island is only five miles in area, and six miles away from Cape Town, and it became a Dutch prison island after many settlement attempts failed, and after that, the British made it a penal (prison) colony. It was also a leper (leprosy victims) colony, and the island for the insane to live. From the 1960s to 1991, it became a prison for maximum security, and most of the prisoners had sentences with political offenses. In 1991, the island closed and all the prisoners were released. Today, it is now a museum, and Robben Island shows future generations what prisoners went through.
In 1948, the National Party became the leading power in the government and it instituted a very strict policy of segregation called apartheid.

The South African natives were allowed to own very little land, but believe it or not, they made up over 70% of the population!
The African Natives were relocated to territories called homelands. They were assigned to specific regions based on their ethnic group. Just to go to work, the natives were required to have passbooks to leave their homeland region. The European invaders controlled the lives of South African natives with segregation everywhere.
The African National Congress and the Pan-Africanist Congress worked tirelessly to tell the government and the international confraternity to place an embargo on the Union of South Africa. An embargo is a turndown for one country to trade with another nation.
In the fullness of time, the South African government was required to make some replacements, invalidating some apartheid laws and emancipating political prisoners.
In the year 1994, Nelson Mandela, the first President of South Africa and the leader of the African National Congress, got elected when all the multi-racial people in South Africa voted in their elections for who? Nelson Mandela.
In 1997, the famous Lockerbie trial occurred. On December 21, 1988, Pan American World Airways Flight 103 was bombed over Lockerbie in the United Kingdom which killed 259 passengers and crew, along with 11 people on the ground. Three years later, two Libyans were convicted for the bombing. In 1992, then-President Mandela proposed for US President George H.W. Bush to have the Lockerbie Trial in a third country. Bush acted favorably, and King Juan Carlos I of Spain and President Francois Mitterrand of France agreed too. Long story short, one of the Libyans was found guilty, while the other was innocent.
The South African government wrote a new constitution, created a democracy, and restructured the young nation into 9 provinces (or states), with the intention of giving equality to all South African citizens, white or black.
IN 1999, the South African government created the Truth and Reconciliation Commision, and their task was to correct the wrongdoings of apartheid. It also continues to assist all the families that were victims of apartheid.
After that, South Africa became one of the world's leading nations in political, economic, and social happenings. In 2002, it also helped create the African Union to help with unification in Africa. It is also a leading member of the UN (United Nations).
Two years later, the government issued a mandate to help cut unemployment in the country in half by 2014, and they are currently planning to erase poverty and eliminate inequality by 2030.
South Africa's beauty is attracting visitors because of its showcasing at the 2010 World Cup. Today, South Africa has been thriving, both economically and physically, and all of that is thanks to the freedom fighters that started all of this.





Biography: Desmond Tutu


Desmond Mpilo Tutu was born on October 7, 1931 in Klerksdorp, a city in what was then the Union of South Africa. When he graduated from the University of South Africa in 1954, Tutu taught at a junior college (high school) for a few years. In 1957, he resigned, and Tutu began to study theology (the study of religion), and was ordained as an Anglican priest in London, the capital city of Great Britain.
He began to live in London, and got an M.A. at Kings College from 1972-75. The year after he got his M.A., Tutu was elected bishop of Lesotho, a landlocked country completely surrounded by South Africa. In 1982, he published a collection of some lectures he made called The Divine Intention.
Tutu was a very outspoken critic of the policy of segregation: apartheid. He saw the inequality of the South African natives and the European invaders. He was then appointed as the general secretary of the South African Churches Council, where he became a spokesperson for the rights of his people. He promoted nonviolent resistance, like protests, and his work earned him the Nobel Peace Prize of 1983.
Even after apartheid ended, Tutu continued to work for total equality, and even when he retired, he still worked with a group of elder world leaders to promote solutions for a number of international problems, including climate change, human rights, HIV, AIDS, and poverty.
Desmond Tutu has received many awards other than the Nobel Peace Prize of 1983. In 2009, he received the United States Presidential Medal of Freedom. Four years after that, in 2013, he received the Templeton Prize, a very prestigious award presented to those "who have made an exceptional contribution to affirming life's spiritual dimension."
On December 26, 2021, in Cape Town, South Africa, Desmond Tutu died. Even though he is not with us now, he still lives on, inspiring millions around the world because of his fighting for justice.




Republic of Kenya

The Republic of Kenya has had rough times from the ancient tribes to the current day, but it is still interesting, and it teaches us a lot about African natives' lives.
During the ancient times of Kenya, there were many villages and towns existing in the area for about 4,000 years. This is because coastal settlements in Kenya grew largely due to the Indian Ocean trade with Arabs. During this time, the Bantu (India) also made their arrival in the country, and believe it or not, Swahili, the official language of Kenya, comes from the languages of the Arabs and the Bantu!
Europeans were not only interested in South Africa, they were eyeing Kenya as a stopping point to resupply shipt. The Portuguese struck again when they came to Kenya in 1498. The next thing they did was establish the port of Mombasa, in eastern Kenya. The Imam of Oman (an Arabic country) came into control of Kenya in the 1600s, and it was controlled by Islamic rule until 200 years later, when the British came along and crashed the Muslim party....
In 1885, the leading European nations, including Great Britain, France, and the German Empire met at the Berlin conference, and the main subject was African colonization. During the Berlin Conference, the British received Kenya, and immediately started colonizing it.

By 1895, Kenya became an official colony of Great Britain (Oh no), and they prevented the Asians and Kenyan natives from having a voice in the government (as in pretty much every colony at those times...).
The British sent thousands of railway workers from their fellow colony, the British Raj (India), therefore adding to the Kenyan diversity.
The Kenyans hated the British ruling their country, and all over the world, nationalism, the love for one's country, was on the rise.
A little over 50 years later, in 1952, the Kenyans had had enough. They started the Mau Mau Movement, calling for their country's independence.
There was another factor that influenced Kenya's independence movement, and that was the Pan-African Movement. The Pan-African Movement was the result of the horrible confessions and the African people's desire for independence, and to think of the continent as a homeland. The goal of the Pan-African Movement was to end European colonialism, and to work for the equality of all of Africa.
Finally, on December 12, 1963, Kenya finally had its independence! But the only issue was that there was only one political party, the Kenyan African National Union (or the KANU), so it essentially ruled the nation by the exact same party until the 1990s. KANU amended (or changed) its constitution in 1982 making it a one political party country.
During this time, many fellow African countries pressured the Kenyan government to allow other political parties and to change the constitution. But even though Kenya did change the constitution, KANU was still the most powerful political party in Kenya.
When KANU gave in, one of the newly created political party groups was the National Rainbow Coalition (or the NARC). During the Kenyan presidential election of 2002, the NARC candidate was elected the third president, and during his term in office, more political parties were created.
Today, Kenya plays a very important role in the region of Eastern Africa as a reputation. That reputation is for being one of the most stable countries in the region and in Africa as a whole! That is why Kenya's history plays a very important role in the emerging history of Eastern Africa.




Federal Republic of Nigeria

Nigeria is one of the emerging countries in the African continent, and its history is very prepossessing. Even though their lifestyle has been scarred with European influence (like pretty much every other country colonized at some point), Nigeria has still survived that, and is still alive today.
Throughout the history of Nigeria, it has had various commanding systems, and frequently, foreign traders have been "invading" the country.
The very first merchants to arrive in Nigeria were people coming from the Arabian Peninsula and other parts of North Africa and the Middle East. They brought many products to Nigeria, including gold, ivory, gum, iron, and slaves (the people didn't get the slaves, later, they WERE the slaves). The Arab merchants also brought the Qu'ran (the Islamic holy book) and Islam to the country.
Out of the four Trans-Saharan trade routes, two of them went through Nigeria, so it became a crossroads of the people, goods, and ideas traveling through the African continent.
In the 16th century (the 1500s), European merchants arrived, searching for gold and slave labor (oh no), and for the next three centuries, the human slave trade was controlled near the African shores, and Nigeria was in the middle of all the chaos.
Even after slavery was abolished in Nigeria in the year 1807, the British stayed in Nigeria. Because of the cocoa, groundnuts, rubber, and palm oil that was in the country, the British made permanent settlements there. Because of the Niger River, the British were able to move inland.

Along with the invasion, the British brought missionaries to convert Nigerian natives to Christianity. In the 19th century, slowly but surely, British influence and rule grew in Nigeria.
In the 20th century, the Pan-African movement began as the reaction to European colonization, and for the people to collaborate to improve Africa. Another ambition of the Pan-African Movement was to end European colonization in Africa, and as it spread to Nigeria and the African continent, all of the African colonies began declaring independence.
After the Allies' triumph in World War II, the British gave Nigeria more and more freedom through a chain of constitutions, and Nigeria finally achieved independence on October 1, 1960. However, shortly after, the northern Muslims and the southern Christians declared war on each other, which caused many casualties.

When the Nigerian country was split into twelve regions, the Igbo people of the east took advantage of that, declaring themselves the Independent State of Biafra, which resulted in the Nigerian civil war. By the end of the conflict, the Independent State of Biafra was once again part of Nigeria, although having lots of military coups in the following years.

In 1999, the Nigerian government created a new constitution, and the new government became civilian (meaning there was no military rule). Nigeria is currently experiencing the longest civilian rule period (people who are leaders have not been a military veteran), even though there was violence during the Nigerian elections.
Nigeria's history has been rattled with European influence, but through all of that, Nigeria has survived it, and is still a patriotic country today.



Biography: Wole Soyinka

Akinwande Oluwole Babatunde "Wole" Soyinka was born on July 13, 1934 in Abeokuta, Nigeria. He attended local schools in Nigeria before leaving for the University of Leeds in England to finish his education.
After Soyinka graduated from Leeds, he returned to Nigeria, where he became a playwright. In 1960, he published his first play, A Dance of the Forests, which was a part of his Nigerian independence celebrations.
Soyinka was a very democratic activist for his country, especially when criticizing the authoritarian dictatorships of Africa. In 1967, he was arrested when he criticized the Nigerian Civil War, the conflict that started when the Igbo people revolted against the government. In the course of his 22 months in solitary confinement (1 year and 10 months), Soyinka continued writing his poetry and authored a collection of poems called Poems from Prison. Shortly after he left prison, he wrote a published narrative of his time there called The Man Died in 1972.
14 years later, in 1986, Wole Soyinka became the first black African to win the Nobel Literature Prize. Since then he has written hundreds of plays and novels, and has taught hundreds of art students. 10 years after that, in Emory University in Atlanta, Georgia, he was named Robert W. Woodruff Professor of the Arts. Soyinka has taught at many prestigious universities, such as Cambridge, Yale, Harvard, and Oxford.
He has also received many awards, in addition. Soyinka was named a Distinguished Scholar in Residence at Duke University, and a scholar-in-residence by New York University's Institute of African-American affairs. In December of 2017, Soyinka was awarded the Europe Theatre Prize, in the Special Prize Category for "contributing to the realization of cultural events that promote understanding and the exchange of knowledge between peoples."
Wole Soyinka has been a very influential figure in African literature, and has inspired countless generations of African children to be like him. In the course of history until it ends, there will never be another Wole Soyinka.
